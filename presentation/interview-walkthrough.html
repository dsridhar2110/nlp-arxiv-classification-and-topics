Start here

What this project is for

The problem: research (and any organisation) produces a flood of text — papers, tickets, notes — far too much to read and sort by hand. Two jobs come up again and again: "which bucket does this document belong in?" and "what are the hidden themes across all of them?"

What I built: PaperLens does both on arXiv computer-science papers (2016–2025). Part 1 is a classifier that reads a paper's title or abstract and predicts whether it's about Computational Linguistics. Part 2 is topic modelling that discovers the themes in the whole corpus with no labels at all. A Streamlit app lets a non-technical person see the results and try the classifier.

Why it matters for me: it's my proof that I can take raw, messy text all the way to working NLP — both the supervised side (classification) and the unsupervised side (topic discovery). And it's the same shape as a Siemens service problem: sort incoming service tickets into hardware/software/ calibration, and discover recurring fault themes nobody has labelled yet.

The whole thing, simply

How I built it, from the start

The plain-English version of the process — the technical words are in teal so I remember to actually say them. It's the same process I followed on my other projects: data → clean → model → the extra → the app.

1

The data and what I'm predicting. I started with arXiv CS papers, each with a Title, an Abstract, and yes/no subfield labels. The thing I'm predicting — the target variable — is the label Computational Linguistics: is this paper about it, yes or no. That's a category, not a number — so this is classification, and the score is F1, not RMSE.

2

Cleaning and turning text into numbers. Raw text can't go into a model, so I pre-processed it with spaCy and nltk: lower-cased, removed stop-words ("the", "of"), and lemmatised (reduced words to their root). Then I turned text into numbers two ways: TF-IDF (weight the distinctive words) for the classical models, and word embeddings + padding for the deep models.

3

The models (classification). I compared classical vs deep. Classical: Logistic Regression and SVM on TF-IDF — fast and interpretable. Deep: an RNN and an LSTM in PyTorch — they read the words in order. I ran each on title-only vs abstract-only and on 1,000 vs the full training set, to see what actually moves the score.

4

The extra — topic modelling. The second, unsupervised half: LDA (gensim) to discover the themes across the papers with no labels. I tuned it — 10 vs 40 topics, with vs without bigrams — and judged the quality with a coherence score, then visualised it with pyLDAvis, word clouds and t-SNE.

5

The website. Finally I wrapped it in a Streamlit app so anyone — not just a data person — can see the results and try the classifier on a title or abstract.

The score

What metric we use — and why it's F1, not RMSE

This is a classification project, so the metric is F1 — not RMSE. RMSE measures how far off you are when you predict a number (like a star rating). Here I predict a category (comp-ling: yes/no), so I score it differently.

Precision
Of the papers I flagged as comp-ling, how many really were. High precision = few false alarms.
Recall
Of the papers that truly are comp-ling, how many I caught. High recall = few misses.
F1 the headline
A single number that balances precision and recall (their harmonic mean). I use it because accuracy can be misleading when the classes aren't 50/50 — F1 keeps me honest on both false alarms and misses. My best is F1 ≈ 0.92.
PR curve
A picture of the precision/recall trade-off as I move the decision threshold — so I can pick the operating point I want, not just 0.5.

What every word means

So I can explain it, not just name it
Classification vs regression
Classification predicts a category (comp-ling: yes/no) — scored by F1/accuracy. Regression predicts a number — scored by RMSE. This project is classification.
Supervised vs unsupervised
Supervised = learn from labelled examples (the classifier). Unsupervised = find structure with no labels (the LDA topics). I did both.
TF-IDF features
"Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency." Turns text into numbers by up-weighting words that are distinctive to a document and down-weighting words that appear everywhere.
Word embeddings
Each word becomes a vector of numbers that captures its meaning, so words used similarly sit close together. What the RNN/LSTM read (vs TF-IDF's word-counts).
Logistic Regression & SVM
Two classical classifiers. LogReg draws a weighted line between the classes and is very interpretable; SVM finds the widest-margin boundary. Both strong, fast baselines on TF-IDF.
When do I use each? ▼

Honest headline: on text they're usually neck-and-neck (mine: SVM 0.920, LogReg 0.917). But there are real reasons to prefer one.

Use Logistic Regression when…

  • You want a probability, not just yes/no — "I'm 87% sure it's comp-ling." Needed to rank by confidence or set a cost-based threshold (like Fleet Pulse). SVM gives a score/distance, not a natural probability.
  • You want interpretability — its coefficients say how much each word pushes the prediction, so you can explain it to a stakeholder.

LogReg = the one that gives a confidence % and a reason.

Use SVM (LinearSVC) when…

  • High-dimensional sparse data — like TF-IDF text. This is SVM's home turf; it's fast and excellent on thousands of text features.
  • You care about the borderline cases — it focuses on the trickiest examples (the "support vectors") and draws the widest-margin line, which can generalise a touch better when classes sit close.
  • You don't need probabilities, just the cleanest classification.

SVM = the one that obsesses over the hardest cases and draws the cleanest dividing line.

Why run both? Two strong baselines that agree is a rigour check — mine landed within 0.003 F1 of each other, so I knew the result was robust, not a fluke of one model. If they'd disagreed wildly, that'd be a red flag.

One-liner: LogReg = probabilities + interpretability; SVM = margins + raw accuracy on sparse text. On text, try both — they usually tie.
RNN & LSTM deep
Neural networks that read a sequence word by word. An RNN carries a running memory; an LSTM is an RNN with gates that fix its "forgetting" problem on longer text. Built here in PyTorch.
LDA topics
"Latent Dirichlet Allocation." An unsupervised method that models each document as a mix of hidden topics, and each topic as a distribution over words — so it discovers themes without being told them.
Coherence
A score for how meaningful a discovered topic is — do its top words genuinely belong together. I used it to choose the number of topics.
Regularisation here
For LogReg/SVM I tuned C (L2 regularisation — how hard to penalise big weights, to avoid over-fitting). The deep models use dropout (randomly ignore neurons in training) for the same reason.
NER (Named Entity Recognition) next step
An NLP technique that reads free text and pulls out the specific named things — a person, a date, or in a service setting a component or error-code. Where classification puts the whole document in a bucket, NER acts like a highlighter that extracts the key items inside it — turning "helium level dropping" into a structured signal you can route or link to a part. I didn't build it here; it's the natural next step for service tickets.
Precision · Recall · F1
Precision = of what I flagged, how much was right. Recall = of what's truly positive, how much I caught. F1 balances the two — my headline metric.

Why it tied

The most important thing to understand from Phase 2

It's the same reason the classical model won in Phase 1, taken to its conclusion. This task is decided by vocabulary, not context: whether a paper is Computational Linguistics is given away by which words appear — "parser", "corpus", "syntax". A transformer's superpower is understanding meaning in context — negation, word order, ambiguity. But here there's barely any ambiguity to resolve — the words are the answer — so BERT has nothing extra to grab that TF-IDF didn't already have.

The one-liner to say: "BERT shines when meaning depends on context; here the topic is written on the surface in the vocabulary, so a keyword model already captures it, and the transformer has no extra edge. A transformer isn't automatically better — you use it where context matters, and here it doesn't."

Let's talk it through

The Phase 2 story, the way I'd actually say it

"So why did you even bother with BERT?"
Because it's the state-of-the-art for text, and the honest thing is to try it, not assume. BERT understands the meaning of words in context, not just which words are present — so on paper it should be the stronger tool. I fine-tuned DistilBERT on the same task to actually check whether it helped.

"And what happened to the F1?"
Almost nothing changed. BERT scored 0.916; the classical TF-IDF model scored 0.917 on the same data — basically identical (and the full-data classical model was 0.920). So BERT didn't get worse — it just didn't get better. The fancy model and the simple model landed in the exact same place.

"Then why didn't the fancy model win?"
Because this is a vocabulary problem, not a context problem. Whether a paper is Computational Linguistics is basically decided by which words show up — "parser", "corpus", "syntax". TF-IDF turns those words straight into numbers, so the simple model already has the answer. There's no hidden meaning to decode — so BERT's real talent (reading context) just sits unused. When the words are the answer, a keyword model is all you need.

When WOULD BERT have won?

A different dataset where context — not keywords — decides it

A great follow-up to be ready for: "give me a task where the transformer would have beaten TF-IDF." Here's the cleanest example.

Imagine the input isn't "title + abstract → topic", but customer feedback → is this positive or negative (that's called sentiment analysis). Now the same words can mean opposite things, because meaning depends on order and on little words like "not":

"The scanner is reliable." positive
Words: scanner, reliable.
"The scanner is not reliable." negative
Almost the same words — just one "not" — but the opposite meaning.

TF-IDF would be fooled here — it just sees two nearly identical bags of words ("scanner", "reliable") and can't tell them apart; to it, "not" is only one more token. BERT would nail it, because it reads the words in context and understands that "not" flips the whole meaning. That's exactly where a transformer earns its keep — negation, sarcasm, word order, long-range meaning. My topic task had none of that; sentiment is full of it.

The rule to remember: use TF-IDF / keywords when the answer is in which words appear (topics, spam, subject tagging). Reach for a transformer when the answer depends on what the words mean together (sentiment, intent, negation). For Siemens: classifying a ticket's subject → keywords are fine; judging a customer's satisfaction tone, or whether an engineer's note says a fault did or didn't recur → that's transformer territory.

What a transformer / BERT actually is

Plain-English, so it's study-ready
Transformer
A neural-network design that reads all the words at once and lets each word look at every other word to decide what matters — instead of shuffling through them one at a time like an RNN. That's why it handles long text and meaning far better.
Attention the key idea
The mechanism inside a transformer: for each word, it works out which other words are relevant and weights them. "Bank" near "river" vs near "money" gets understood differently — that's attention resolving context.
BERT
A transformer pre-trained on a huge pile of text so it already "knows language", which you then fine-tune on your small labelled task. You inherit the language understanding and only teach the specifics. DistilBERT is a smaller, faster BERT — what I used.
Fine-tuning
Taking the pre-trained model and training it a little more on your labelled data, so it adapts to your task. That's literally what I did here for Computational-Linguistics classification.
Why it beats TF-IDF / RNNs in general
TF-IDF is order-blind word-counts; RNNs read in sequence but forget over long spans; the transformer sees everything at once and understands context. So it usually wins when meaning depends on context — just not on a task like this one, where the vocabulary alone decides it.

"That's the biggest practical difference. With LDA I have to pick the number of topics (K) upfront — I set 25. So it's not just scoring higher; it's finding the natural structure and being more granular — it cleanly separated things like speech recognition from reinforcement learning.
